# What Real People Actually Ask About Permits

Research compiled 2026-03-02 from Reddit, forums, and SD city data.

## The User's Mental Journey (in order)

### Stage 0: "Do I even need a permit for this?"
This is the FIRST question. Before anything else. And there's no good answer anywhere.
- "I'm replacing my kitchen cabinets and adding an outlet. Do I need a permit?"
- "If I don't mess with structural, plumbing, or electrical... am I fine?"
- "My contractor didn't mention permits. Should I be worried?"
- Cosmetic (paint, flooring, cabinets) = no. Structural/electrical/plumbing = yes. But the line is blurry.
- **Fear:** Unpermitted work gets discovered when you sell. Buyers' inspectors find it. You either tear it out or get retroactive permits (expensive, uncertain).

### Stage 1: "What am I actually allowed to do on my property?"
Once they know they need a permit, they have NO idea what their property allows.
- "I'm in RM-2-4. What does that even mean?"
- "Can I build an ADU? How big? Where on my lot?"
- "I heard there are overlay zones but I don't know what mine are"
- "What's the difference between an ADU, JADU, and garage conversion?"

**The options that change everything for ADUs:**
- **ADU vs JADU**: JADU is ≤500 sq ft, must be within existing structure or attached garage. ADU can be detached, up to 1,200 sq ft. You can build BOTH on the same lot (ADU + JADU).
- **Attached vs detached**: Attached = built onto existing house. Detached = separate structure in backyard.
- **Garage conversion**: Convert existing garage to living space. Cheaper, faster, no new foundation. But you lose parking.
- **New construction vs conversion**: Converting existing space = way cheaper, fewer requirements, no size cap (conversion not subject to 1,200 sq ft max).
- **Size thresholds that change fees**: Under 500 sq ft = exempt from school fees in many cases. Under 750 sq ft = reduced impact fees. Over 800 sq ft = must comply with FAR (floor area ratio).
- **County has FREE pre-approved ADU plans**: Skip/reduce architectural costs and permit time. Most people don't know this exists. URL: sandiegocounty.gov/content/sdc/pds/bldg/adu_plans.html

### Stage 2: "How much is this actually going to cost me — ALL in?"
Not just permit fees. TOTAL cost. This is where people get blindsided.
- Permit/plan check fees: $6,500 (400 sq ft) to $21,000+ (750 sq ft)
- Architectural plans: $5,000-$15,000
- Construction: $250-$400/sq ft in San Diego ($125k-$300k for typical ADU)
- Real example from Reddit: 590 sq ft ADU in uptown SD = **$327,756 total** including solar, landscaping, garage repair
- Another example: 326 sq ft ADU = ~$175k all in
- High-end example: 700 sq ft ADU + 270 sq ft JADU = started at $350k, ended at $650k (Covid, upgrades, unforeseen costs)
- **The fee confusion**: "I called the county but can't get a straight answer. I've heard everything from $5,000 to $60,000."
- **DSD overcharging**: Multiple documented cases of DSD incorrectly overcharging $10,000+ on ADU permits. People who didn't know better just paid it.
- **Hidden costs**: Plan runner fees ($200-600 if you don't DIY), resubmission fees for each correction round, contractor delays during permit wait

### Stage 3: "How long is this going to take?"
- "How long does it take to get building permits approved through the City of San Diego?"
- Answer: it depends on project complexity, application completeness, and DSD's current backlog
- Real timelines: Simple remodel 30-60 days first review. ADU 60-150+ days. Then 2-5 rounds of corrections at 3 weeks each.
- **The emotional gut punch**: "Originally supposed to be a 6 month job, ended up being 11 months"
- **No in-person resolution since COVID**: "You used to be able to walk in, sit there, meet with several people, and potentially get your permit within a day or two. That process right now takes upwards three or four months."

### Stage 4: "What do I actually need to submit?"
- This is where the process becomes impenetrable for a normal person
- "Reach out to the building department and ask them what you need" ← this is the actual advice
- The city's Information Bulletin 400 is 10+ pages of dense legal text about ADU rules
- People don't know what a "site plan" vs "floor plan" vs "elevation" is
- They don't know what Title 24 is
- They don't know what a stormwater checklist is
- **The plan runner**: Some people pay $200-600 for a "plan runner" to navigate the submission process for them. The fact that this job EXISTS tells you everything about how broken the process is.

### Stage 5: "What decisions do I need to make that affect everything downstream?"
For ADU specifically, these are the branching decisions:
1. **ADU or JADU or both?** (changes size, cost, permit type, whether you need new construction)
2. **Detached or attached or garage conversion?** (changes foundation requirements, cost, setback rules)
3. **What size?** (under 500 sq ft vs 500-750 vs 750-1200 — each bracket has different fee/rule implications)
4. **Using pre-approved county plans or custom?** (saves thousands in architect fees and months in review time)
5. **Will you live in the main house or the ADU?** (owner-occupancy requirements for JADUs)
6. **Do you want to rent it?** (affects how you design it, whether you need separate utilities, etc.)

## The Core User Questions (ranked by frequency/urgency)

1. **Do I even need a permit for this?** (most common, first question)
2. **How much will the WHOLE thing cost?** (fees + construction + architecture + surprises)
3. **What are my options?** (ADU vs JADU, attached vs detached, size brackets)
4. **How long will it take?** (including the permit process, not just construction)
5. **What documents do I need?** (and what even ARE these documents?)
6. **Am I going to get screwed by something I don't know about?** (overlays, historic review, fees)
7. **Can I do this myself or do I need to hire someone?** (architect, contractor, plan runner)
8. **What can go wrong?** (corrections, overcharges, unexpected requirements)
9. **Has anyone else done this?** (real examples, real costs, real timelines)
10. **What happens if I just skip the permit?** (consequences, risks at sale time)

## Key Insight for Our Product

The user journey is NOT linear. It's a decision tree:
- "Do I need a permit?" → if yes →
- "What are my options?" → pick one →
- "What will it cost + how long?" → decide to proceed →
- "What do I need to submit?" → prepare →
- "Submit and wait"

**We should build the tool around this decision tree, not a linear phase list.**

The EXPLORATION phase (stages 0-3) is where the product has the most value. By the time someone is at "what do I submit" they usually have an architect handling it. The magic is in the earlier stages where people are alone, confused, and making decisions that affect everything downstream.
